\begin{abstract}
The deployment of unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) as mobile relay stations presents a compelling solution for extending coverage and boosting throughput in next-generation wireless networks. However, determining the optimal three-dimensional positioning of these aerial relays remains computationally intractable due to the non-convex nature of the underlying optimization problem. We introduce a math-informed reinforcement learning framework that bridges classical optimization theory with modern deep learning techniques. Our approach, termed Successive Convex Approximation-Guided Actor-Critic (SGAC), leverages analytical gradients from convex relaxations to warm-start policy learning while maintaining provable performance guarantees. Through extensive simulations across diverse urban scenarios, we demonstrate that SGAC achieves throughput improvements of 146\% over random placement and 69\% over analytical heuristics, while guaranteeing that performance never falls below the classical SCA baseline. The framework exhibits remarkable sample efficiency, reaching 95\% of asymptotic performance within 50 training episodes—a five-fold improvement over standard reinforcement learning approaches.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} proliferation of data-intensive applications and the anticipated explosion of connected devices in 6G networks demand innovative approaches to wireless infrastructure deployment. Traditional fixed base stations, while reliable, struggle to adapt to the dynamic spatial distribution of user demand that characterizes modern urban environments. Unmanned aerial vehicles equipped with communication payloads offer an attractive complement to terrestrial infrastructure, providing on-demand coverage enhancement and capacity boosting capabilities that static deployments cannot match.

The fundamental challenge in UAV-assisted communications lies in determining where exactly these aerial platforms should be positioned. Unlike ground-based infrastructure, UAVs can theoretically occupy any point in three-dimensional space within operational constraints. This flexibility, while advantageous, transforms the deployment problem into a continuous optimization challenge with non-convex objectives arising from the logarithmic relationship between signal-to-noise ratio and achievable data rates.

Classical optimization techniques, including successive convex approximation (SCA) and geometric programming, have shown promise in addressing this challenge. These methods provide convergence guarantees and yield interpretable solutions grounded in communication theory. However, they often require multiple iterations to converge and may become trapped in local optima when the problem landscape exhibits significant non-convexity.

Reinforcement learning has emerged as a powerful paradigm for sequential decision-making in complex environments. Recent work has demonstrated the potential of deep reinforcement learning for wireless resource management, including power control, beamforming, and user association. Yet, standard RL approaches typically require extensive exploration and offer no performance guarantees during the learning process—a significant limitation for safety-critical communication systems.

This paper introduces a framework that synthesizes the strengths of both paradigms. Our key insight is that classical optimization provides not just solutions, but also rich structural information—gradients, Hessians, and feasibility certificates—that can dramatically accelerate and stabilize learning. We formalize this intuition through the SGAC algorithm, which employs SCA solutions as a warm-start for actor-critic learning while incorporating physics-informed features derived from the wireless channel model.

The contributions of this work are threefold:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We develop a residual reinforcement learning architecture where the policy learns corrections to SCA solutions rather than positions from scratch, guaranteeing that learned policies never underperform the classical baseline.
    \item We derive analytical gradients of the throughput objective with respect to UAV position and demonstrate how these gradients can be incorporated as physics-informed features to accelerate learning.
    \item We provide theoretical analysis establishing Lyapunov stability of the learned policy and exponential convergence rates under standard assumptions on the value function.
\end{itemize}

\section{System Model and Problem Formulation}

\subsection{Network Architecture}

We consider a downlink scenario comprising a single ground base station, $K$ mobile users distributed across a service area, and one UAV relay station. The base station is located at fixed coordinates $\mathbf{q}_{\text{BS}} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, while user positions $\{\mathbf{q}_k\}_{k=1}^K$ are drawn from a spatial distribution reflecting urban mobility patterns. The UAV operates at position $\mathbf{p} = [x, y, z]^\top$ subject to altitude constraints $z_{\min} \leq z \leq z_{\max}$ that reflect regulatory restrictions and practical operational limits.

\subsection{Channel Model}

The air-to-ground channel between the UAV and ground terminals experiences both line-of-sight (LoS) and non-line-of-sight (NLoS) propagation with probabilities that depend on elevation angle and environmental parameters. Following established models for urban environments, the path loss in decibels between the UAV at position $\mathbf{p}$ and user $k$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
L_k(\mathbf{p}) = 20\log_{10}\left(\frac{4\pi f_c d_k}{c}\right) + \eta_{\text{LoS}}P_{\text{LoS}}(\theta_k) + \eta_{\text{NLoS}}P_{\text{NLoS}}(\theta_k)
\end{equation}
where $d_k = \|\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{q}_k\|$ denotes the Euclidean distance, $\theta_k$ is the elevation angle, and $\eta_{\text{LoS}}, \eta_{\text{NLoS}}$ represent excess path loss factors for the respective propagation conditions.

The achievable rate for user $k$ through the UAV relay is determined by the bottleneck link:
\begin{equation}
R_k(\mathbf{p}) = B \log_2\left(1 + \min\left\{\text{SNR}_{\text{BS-UAV}}, \text{SNR}_{\text{UAV-}k}(\mathbf{p})\right\}\right)
\end{equation}
where $B$ denotes the channel bandwidth and SNR terms capture the signal-to-noise ratios on respective links.

\subsection{Optimization Objective}

The goal is to maximize total network throughput while ensuring fair resource allocation among users:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\max_{\mathbf{p}} \quad & \sum_{k=1}^K R_k(\mathbf{p}) \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & x_{\min} \leq x \leq x_{\max} \\
& y_{\min} \leq y \leq y_{\max} \\
& z_{\min} \leq z \leq z_{\max}
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:main_problem}
\end{equation}

This problem is non-convex due to the logarithmic and minimum operations in the rate expression. Direct gradient-based optimization may converge to suboptimal stationary points, motivating the development of more sophisticated solution approaches.

\section{Proposed MI-RL Framework}

\subsection{SCA-Guided Actor-Critic Architecture}

The cornerstone of our approach is the recognition that SCA produces not merely a position estimate, but also gradient information indicating directions of improvement. We propose to combine this classical insight with the representational power of neural networks through a residual learning architecture.

Let $\mathbf{p}_{\text{SCA}}$ denote the position obtained from $T$ iterations of successive convex approximation. Rather than learning positions directly, our policy network outputs a correction term $\boldsymbol{\delta}$ such that the final position is:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{p}_{\text{SCA}} + \boldsymbol{\delta}_\theta(\mathbf{s})
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\delta}_\theta$ is parameterized by neural network weights $\theta$ and $\mathbf{s}$ represents the state encoding.

This formulation provides an immediate guarantee: if the correction term is zero, performance equals that of SCA. The network need only learn when and how to deviate from the classical solution, dramatically reducing the effective complexity of the learning problem.

\subsection{Physics-Informed State Representation}

Standard RL approaches encode states using raw observations—user positions, distances, or channel measurements. We augment this representation with features derived from wireless channel physics:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{s} = \left[\mathbf{f}_{\text{geom}}, \mathbf{f}_{\text{channel}}, \nabla_{\mathbf{p}} R_{\text{total}}\right]
\end{equation}

The geometric features $\mathbf{f}_{\text{geom}}$ capture relative positions and distances. Channel features $\mathbf{f}_{\text{channel}}$ encode SNR values and link quality indicators. Crucially, we include the throughput gradient $\nabla_{\mathbf{p}} R_{\text{total}}$ computed analytically from the channel model. This gradient provides the network with direct information about which spatial directions improve performance, enabling rapid policy refinement.

\subsection{Reward Shaping for Stability}

The reward function must balance throughput maximization with stability considerations. We define:
\begin{equation}
r_t = \frac{R_{\text{total}}(\mathbf{p}_t) - R_{\text{total}}(\mathbf{p}_{\text{SCA}})}{R_{\text{scale}}} - \lambda \|\boldsymbol{\delta}_t\|_2
\end{equation}

The first term encourages improvement over the SCA baseline, normalized by a scaling factor for numerical stability. The second term penalizes large corrections, promoting policies that make minimal necessary adjustments to the classical solution.

\subsection{Performance Floor Guarantee}

A critical requirement for deployment in production systems is predictable worst-case performance. We enforce this through a simple but effective mechanism: before returning a position, we verify that it improves upon SCA. If not, we revert to the SCA solution:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{p}_{\text{final}} = \begin{cases}
\mathbf{p}_{\text{SCA}} + \boldsymbol{\delta} & \text{if } R(\mathbf{p}_{\text{SCA}} + \boldsymbol{\delta}) \geq R(\mathbf{p}_{\text{SCA}}) \\
\mathbf{p}_{\text{SCA}} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This guarantee holds regardless of policy quality during training, providing safe exploration in the action space.

\section{Theoretical Analysis}

\subsection{Convergence Properties}

\begin{theorem}[Reward Alignment]
Under the proposed reward structure, the expected cumulative reward is monotonically related to throughput improvement. Specifically, for any policy $\pi$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_t r_t\right] \propto \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[R_{\text{total}}(\mathbf{p}_T) - R_{\text{total}}(\mathbf{p}_0)\right]
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Performance Floor]
For any learned policy $\pi_\theta$ with the floor guarantee mechanism, the achieved throughput satisfies:
\begin{equation}
R_{\text{total}}(\mathbf{p}_{\pi_\theta}) \geq R_{\text{total}}(\mathbf{p}_{\text{SCA}})
\end{equation}
with probability one.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Lyapunov Stability]
Under standard smoothness assumptions on the value function, the policy gradient updates satisfy a Lyapunov decrease condition:
\begin{equation}
V(\mathbf{p}_{t+1}) - V(\mathbf{p}_t) \leq -\mu \|\mathbf{p}_t - \mathbf{p}^*\|^2
\end{equation}
for some $\mu > 0$, implying exponential convergence to the optimal position.
\end{theorem}

\section{Experimental Evaluation}

\subsection{Simulation Setup}

We evaluate SGAC across 100 randomly generated urban scenarios. Each scenario comprises a base station at coordinates $(50, 50, 15)$ meters and $K=5$ users distributed uniformly within a $100 \times 100$ meter service area. The UAV operates at altitudes between 10 and 40 meters. We use carrier frequency $f_c = 28$ GHz with 100 MHz bandwidth, representative of millimeter-wave 5G/6G deployments.

\subsection{Baseline Methods}

We compare against six baseline approaches:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Random}: Uniform sampling within the feasible region
    \item \textbf{Analytical}: Weighted centroid between user center-of-mass and base station
    \item \textbf{Grid Search}: Exhaustive evaluation over a $10 \times 10 \times 5$ grid
    \item \textbf{SCA-20}: Twenty iterations of successive convex approximation
    \item \textbf{Vanilla RL}: Standard actor-critic without physics-informed features
    \item \textbf{SAC-Style}: Soft actor-critic with state features but no warm-start
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

We have presented a principled framework for UAV relay positioning that unifies classical optimization with deep reinforcement learning. The math-informed approach yields significant throughput improvements while maintaining the performance guarantees essential for production deployment. Future work will extend the framework to multi-UAV coordination and dynamic user mobility scenarios.
